Results:

Textual analysis of the publications of the Sherlock Holmes stories reveals a lot about how women were represented in pop culture during the time and why that representation might have changed. Although Sherlock Holmes was created by a singular man, and thus is not representative of everyone's ideas regarding women, because the stories were so widespread and popular, the stories' representation of women can still reveal a lot about prevailing societal views.

This visualization showcases the total number of words denoting men or women that appeared in the publications of Sherlock Holmes stories. There are two notable things that this visualization reveals. The first of which is that men have been included in the Sherlock Holmes stories much more often than women, with words denoting men being included a total of 26,287 times as opposed to the 6,849 times words denoting women have been included. However, even though women were still mentioned less than men at 1,630 as opposed to men's mentions of 3,651, this is a great improvement from the Sherlock Holmes stories released during the social purity movement.

This visualization showcases the T-Scores of words denoting stereotypical female emotions in relation to men and women. There are two important insights to be gained from this visualization. The first of which is that in every yearly category, women's T-Scores are at least four times higher than men's, showcasing that women are often represented in the context of their female emotions. The second useful insight is that in the period after the social purity movement, women show up in context of words regarding stereotypical female emotions much less often.

These visualizations showcase how often words relating to women show up in context of words relating to marital expectations. Although over the total years, women do show up in the context of their marital expectations 144 times, that is only 2% of their total mentions. Even though the percentage of times that references to females are made in context to marital expectations is low and not particularly significant to begin with, there was a slight improvement after the social purity movement's conclusion in how often women were mentioned in the context of marital expectations, with the percentage dropping from 2.49% to .86%.

This visualization showcases how often women show up in context with words denoting irrationality. Although men are relatively likely to show up in context with words denoting irrationality themselves, women are still almost twice as likely to be mentioned in context with irrationality. However, this visualization is a bit odd in the fact that instead of improving after the social purity movement, women are actually more likely to show up in context with words denoting irrationality in the period after the social purity movement.

These visualizations showcase the comparison of how often men and women appeared in the context of the other sex over the three yearly periods the data was divided into. The data represented in these visualizations demonstrates how often women were mentioned in the context of men, with almost 15% of total mentions of women in all publications showing up in the context of men. This is a stark difference between men who are only mentioned in the context of women 3% of the total times that they are mentioned. Furthermore, these visualizations illustrate that women were less likely to be mentioned in the context of men after the conlusion of the social purity movement, with the percentage of the times they were mentioned near men dropping from almost 16% during the movement to about 9% after the movement.
